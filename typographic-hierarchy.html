Typographic Hierarchy
Print/ Illustration
2012
Individual Project

About the Process

By using different weight, flushing, line-spacing and other elements, I explored different ways of arranging the text so that it helps the readers to read without “reading”. There are four parts, 10 small assignments, to this assignment and in different parts, I was only allowed to use limited elements to being out the hierarchy of the text.


Part One

1 Linespacing

Select one weight (Helvetica Neue light, roman, medium, bold). Set all the type in that weight. Insert one full linespace between any two lines of type, once or more than once, throughout all the lines. You may not insert more (or less) than one full linespace between any two lines of type.


2 Typographic weights

Select any two weights (Helvetica Neue light, roman, medium). Set all the type in a combination of those two weights. Next, explore all four stroke weights in combination. No linespacing.


3 Horizontal shift

Select one weight (Helvetica Neue light, roman, medium, bold). Shift lines of type horizontally left or right, resulting in only two flush-left margins. No linespacing.


4 Horizontal shift

Select one weight (Helvetica Neue light, roman, medium, bold). Shift lines of type horizontally left or right, resulting in three flush-left margins. No linespacing.


Part Two

5 Linespacing & typographic weight

Select any two weights (Helvetica Neue light, roman, medium, bold). Insert one full linespace between any two lines of type, once or more than once, throughout all the lines. You may not insert more (or less) than one full linespace between any two lines of type.


6 Typographic weights & horizontal shift

Select any two weights (Helvetic Neue light, roman, medium, bold). Shift lines of type horizontally left or right, resulting in only two flush-left margins. No linespacing.


7 Horizontal shift & linespacing

Select one weight (Helvetica Neue light, roman, medium, bold). Insert one full linespace between any two lines of type, once or more than once, throughout all the lines. Shift lines of type horizontally left or right, resulting in only two flush-left margins.


Part Three

8 Size Change & Typographic Weight

Select any two weights (Helvetica Neue light, roman, medium, bold). Use a change of point size to emphasize hierarchical differences. You may change the order of the information, if you wish. Horizontal shifts and linespacing are optional. This is your call. Think carefully.


9 Graphic Elements: Rules, Bullets, Geometric Shapes

Start with a complete solution to one of the previous eight exercises; add any of these graphic elements: rules (your choice of weight), bullets (dots, your choice of size and weight), and geometric shapes. Stay with black, white and values of grey (% of black) in your graphic elements, bullets, rules and type. Again, this is your call. Try a range of starting points, not just one. Be adventurous and try many ideas. Explore!


10 Color & Everything Before

Start with a complete solution to one of the previous nine exercises; explore how color can emphasize or complement the typography. Consider color in shapes behind the type; color in the type; color in limited amounts; color all over the piece; try analogous color palette with one contrasting hue, complimentary colors, try a strong palette, an ugly palette, a cool palette, a loud palette etc.

The original idea starts with an axis. It is a colored extended version from part 9. The maroon color is the official CMU color and since this is a poster that is suppose to represent CMU to the public, I thought it was necessary to use the school color.The good thing about using a directed vertical axis is that it guides people’s attention upwards. I integrated a “break” in the line right where the topics are so that people’s attention will be diverted to the topic while they are following the vertical axis.I also tried to insert text to the left of the axis so that the entire page looks more balanced.


From that idea, I tried to make the axis more realistic. If I can build a 3D space with a z-axis in it, why not? That’s the inspiration for the second set of posters.The hard part about drawing out a 3D space is that the lines should be not too obvious to be the center of attention but at the same time it should be noticeable from a distance. At first, I tried to use grids to show distance and space but I soon realize that the grids is a great distraction. Then, I simplified the grids to just lines yet it seems to lose the depth effect and the chunks of information looks like they are floating in mid air.Grids are pretty hard to implement and at the end, I didn’t use this design.

Failed to use grids for depth perception, I decided to use another visual trick “Good Continuation”. The idea is that we tend to connect breaking lines that form a certain regular or familiar shape. If only corners of a square are present, we still tend to see a square due to “Good Continuation”. Another benefit of using corners rather than a real square is that the “invisible” square can separate information without making the viewer feel claustrophobic.At this point, I was bored with one single color so I decided to use complementary colors to make the information even clearer.The downside of this design is that there is no layer of information. Everything on the page seems flat and of equal importance (except for the title part which is my favorite thing about this poster).

This is only a crazy idea I had while brainstorming for more designs.

Still unhappy with my poster, I chose to explore other options. One of the ideas I had was to use Cartesian Coordinate System. With tilted coordinates and the letters, the poster seems more interactive than the previous designs.The poster on the left side is the poster that I brought to the critique. Some of the feedback include comments such as “it is hard to read the poster” and “the axis looks unnatural”.So for the final design, I focused on the comments I got back from the critique. I redrew the coordinate system and rewrote all of the words. I tilted the entire poster with care so that it looks crooked while it is still easy to read. I also change the lines to words to so that the viewers have a surprise when they walk close to see the poster. “Oh, the lines are words! How interesting!”
